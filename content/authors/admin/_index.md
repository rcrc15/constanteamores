I am a PhD student funded by the CDT-TSM and BP-ICAM scholarships at Imperial College London. My interests are in applied mathematics and in particular fluid dynamics problems involving moving interfaces. I use modelling, analysis and computations to study the presence of Marangoni forces due to surface tension gradients resulting from the presence of soluble or insoluble surfactants. Mathematically, the models are reduced systems of the full equations of motion (the Navier-Stokes equations) and typically lead to one- or two-dimensional nonlinear PDEs.

The overarching theme of my PhD thesis is to study the physics of interface breakup and coalescence, which exemplify situations wherein the interface undergoes topological transitions. These transitions in- volve the development of singularities where interfacial distances vanish and velocity fields diverge, requiring highly-specialised numerics. As such, my thesis tries to answer questions regarding turbulence, fluid me- chanics, applied-maths and applied physics. The fundamental questions explored in my dissertation is the role of vorticity in the dynamics of turbulent jets and the effect of chemical surfactants on the interfacial phenomena. Turbulent jets are influenced by a range of multi-scale physics; this includes the interaction of turbulence with interfaces (creating cascades of motion featuring a large separation of scales), capillarity, viscosity (and complex rheology), and gravity, as well as heat transfer and phase change. The presence of surface-active agents (surfactants), either as contaminants, pathogens, or additives, also influences the flow dynamics through the generation of Marangoni stresses adding layers of complexity to the phenomena. The multi-scale nature of the flow, and the tremendously complex interfacial topology, however, complicates the scrutiny of multiphase flows experimentally even in the absence of additives. Thus, elucidating the influ- ence of additives on these flows requires the development of theoretical and computational tools to enable high-fidelity simulations to be deployed to drive experiment design.
